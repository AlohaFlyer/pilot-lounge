Job Seeking & CareersThe road from first lesson to the flight deck

There is a seat out there with your name on it. This is the map: the ratings you stack, the hours that gate each step, the ways pilots build those hours, and how the hiring actually works. Six airline captains built this lounge to pay it back. Here is the part where we hand you the ladder.

The ratings ladder

  • STUDENTStudent pilot. Where everyone starts. Solo with your instructor's endorsement, learn the airplane and the airspace, work toward first solo and the written.
  • PRIVATE (PPL)Carry passengers, not for hire. The foundation certificate. Fly friends and family, rent airplanes, travel. The whole private ACS is what the podcast season covers.
  • INSTRUMENT (IR)Fly in the clouds. An add-on rating, not a certificate. Lets you fly in instrument meteorological conditions under IFR. The single biggest jump in capability and safety you will make.
  • COMMERCIAL (CPL)Get paid to fly. Higher precision standards and the door to working as a pilot. Most career flying starts here.
  • CFI / CFII / MEITeach what you know. The flight instructor certificates. The classic way to build hours while earning, and the fastest way to truly master the material, because you have to explain it.
  • MULTI (AMEL)Two engines. The multi-engine class rating, required for almost every airline and most turbine jobs. Often added right before or during time building.
  • ATPThe top certificate. Required to act as pilot in command (and, since 2013, as a first officer) of an airliner. The destination rung of the ladder.

The hours that gate each step

These are the headline FAR Part 61 minimums. They are floors, not targets, and the totals you actually need to be competitive are usually higher. Always confirm against current 14 CFR Part 61.

The career path

Where the seats are

The traditional ladder runs regional first, then a major or legacy carrier, building turbine and multi-crew time along the way. But it is not the only route, and not even always the highest paid one. Cargo (freight) and corporate and fractional flying are full careers in their own right, and plenty of pilots never fly a passenger airline at all.

Part 121 is the scheduled airlines and major cargo. Part 135 is on-demand charter and commuter. Part 91 is general operating rules, including most corporate and private flying. Good careers live in all of them.

Legacy carriers

The big three full-service network airlines: American, Delta, and United. Largest fleets, global networks, widebody flying, and the top of most pay scales.

Other majors and low-cost carriers

  • Southwest, Alaska (now including Hawaiian), and JetBlue are large established mainline carriers.
  • Spirit, Frontier, Allegiant, Sun Country, Breeze, and Avelo are the low-cost and ultra-low-cost carriers.

Cargo and freight

  • FedEx Express and UPS Airlines are the cargo majors, with pay and benefits that rival or beat the passenger legacies.
  • Atlas Air, Kalitta Air, Western Global, ABX Air, and Amerijet are ACMI and supplemental freight operators, a common turbine and widebody way in.

Regional airlines

Where most airline careers begin. They fly as American Eagle, Delta Connection, and United Express:

  • SkyWest is the largest US regional, 500+ aircraft, flying for all three legacies.
  • Republic Airways flies about 200 aircraft for American, Delta, and United (merged with Mesa in 2025).
  • Envoy Air, PSA Airlines, and Piedmont Airlines are American's wholly-owned American Eagle carriers, with a flow to American mainline.
  • Endeavor Air is Delta's wholly-owned Delta Connection carrier, with a flow to Delta.
  • Horizon Air is Alaska's regional and the Ascend Pilot Academy destination.
  • GoJet, Air Wisconsin, and CommutAir round out the United Express and independent regionals.

Corporate and fractional

NetJets and Flexjet are the big fractional operators, and thousands of company flight departments fly Part 91 and Part 135 in everything from turboprops to large-cabin jets. Excellent equipment, mentorship, and increasingly competitive pay, with widely varying schedules.

Australia and New Zealand

The ladder rhymes, under different regulators. In Australia the regulator is CASA: you build from a CPL through ATPL theory, a multi-engine command instrument rating, and an MCC course. The Qantas Group Pilot Academy (Toowoomba) is an ab-initio school whose strong graduates are invited to a Qantas Group assessment for QantasLink, Jetstar, or Network Aviation. Mainline carriers are Qantas, Jetstar, and Virgin Australia, with regionals like QantasLink, Rex (Regional Express), and Alliance.

In New Zealand the regulator is the CAA NZ, and the licence path is similar (CPL, then ATPL). Air New Zealand is the flag carrier and runs partner-school and preferred-pathway schemes into its turboprop fleet and on to jets. Other operators include Sounds Air, Originair, Barrier Air, and Air Chathams. A New Zealand licence must be converted to an Australian one before applying to Qantas Group, and vice versa.

US territories: Puerto Rico, USVI, Guam, American Samoa, CNMI

These are US jurisdictions, so the FAA rules and the exact ladder above apply: same certificates, same Part 61 hour gates, same ATP. Local island flying (inter-island hops, Part 135 commuter and cargo) is excellent early experience and time building, and pilots there move into the same regionals, majors, and cargo carriers as the mainland.

Getting hired

  • Keep your records clean and current. A tidy, accurate logbook and a clean certificate file save you at every interview. John would have it laminated.
  • Network early and pay it forward. Most pilots get somewhere because someone took the time on them. Say yes to the hangar conversation. Find a mentor, then become one.
  • Get the ratings in a smart order. Instrument and commercial first, then your instructor certificates if you are building time by teaching. Add multi when the job market wants it.
  • Treat every flight as your reputation. Aviation is small. The way you fly, brief, and behave on the ramp follows you. Stay humble, stay current, pick the safe option every time.
  • Know your numbers cold. Interviews test the same fundamentals this portal drills. Weak on a subject, drill it on the quiz and the games until it sticks.
  • Decide before you need to. Personal minimums, an out, never push it. The pilots who build long careers are the ones who keep showing up, flight after flight, safely.

The ratings ladder

  • STUDENTStudent pilot. Where everyone starts. Solo with your instructor's endorsement, learn the airplane and the airspace, work toward first solo and the written.
  • PRIVATE (PPL)Carry passengers, not for hire. The foundation certificate. Fly friends and family, rent airplanes, travel. The whole private ACS is what the podcast season covers.
  • INSTRUMENT (IR)Fly in the clouds. An add-on rating, not a certificate. Lets you fly in instrument meteorological conditions under IFR. The single biggest jump in capability and safety you will make.
  • COMMERCIAL (CPL)Get paid to fly. Higher precision standards and the door to working as a pilot. Most career flying starts here.
  • CFI / CFII / MEITeach what you know. The flight instructor certificates. The classic way to build hours while earning, and the fastest way to truly master the material, because you have to explain it.
  • MULTI (AMEL)Two engines. The multi-engine class rating, required for almost every airline and most turbine jobs. Often added right before or during time building.
  • ATPThe top certificate. Required to act as pilot in command (and, since 2013, as a first officer) of an airliner. The destination rung of the ladder.

The career path

Regional, then major

The traditional airline ladder runs regional airline first, building turbine and multi-crew experience, then a move to a major or legacy carrier. Plenty of pilots also build careers in Part 135 charter and cargo, Part 91 corporate and fractional flying, and outside the airlines entirely. There is no single right route.

Part 121 is the scheduled airlines. Part 135 is on-demand charter and commuter. Part 91 is general operating rules, including most corporate and private flying. Each has its own quality-of-life, pay, and schedule trade-offs, and good careers live in all three.

Getting hired

  • Keep your records clean and current. A tidy, accurate logbook and a clean certificate file save you at every interview. John would have it laminated.
  • Network early and pay it forward. Most pilots get somewhere because someone took the time on them. Say yes to the hangar conversation. Find a mentor, then become one.
  • Get the ratings in a smart order. Instrument and commercial first, then your instructor certificates if you are building time by teaching. Add multi when the job market wants it.
  • Treat every flight as your reputation. Aviation is small. The way you fly, brief, and behave on the ramp follows you. Stay humble, stay current, pick the safe option every time.
  • Know your numbers cold. Interviews test the same fundamentals this portal drills. Weak on a subject, drill it on the quiz and the games until it sticks.
  • Decide before you need to. Personal minimums, an out, never push it. The pilots who build long careers are the ones who keep showing up, flight after flight, safely.
